The tricky part is showing that the operation is well defined. Suppose \(\theta_1, \theta_2, t_1\text{,}\), and \(t_2\) are in \(\R\text{,}\), with \(e^{i\theta_1}=e^{it_1}\) and \(e^{i\theta_2}=e^{it_2}\text{.}\). We want to show that \begin{equation*} e^{i\theta_1}e^{i\theta_2}=e^{it_1}e^{it_2}, \end{equation*} i.e., that \begin{equation*} e^{i(\theta_1+\theta_2)}=e^{i(t_1+t_2)}. \end{equation*}
