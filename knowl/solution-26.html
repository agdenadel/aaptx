Solution3.4.6.1

  1. Since \(G\simeq G'\text{,}\) there exists some isomorphism \(\phi:G\to G'\text{.}\) Let \(x,y\in G'\text{.}\) Since \(\phi\) is onto, there exist \(a,b\in G\) such that \(\phi(a)=x\) and \(\phi(b)=y\text{.}\) So \begin{align*} xy\amp =\phi(a)\phi(b)\amp \amp\\ \amp =\phi(ab)\amp \amp \text{ (since \(\phi\) is a homomorphism) }\\ \amp =\phi(ba) \amp \amp \text{ (since \(G\) is abelian) }\\ \amp =\phi(b)\phi(a)\\ \amp =yx. \end{align*} Thus, \(G'\) is abelian.

  2. Let \(G=\{I_2\}\) (under multiplication) and let \(G'=GL(2,\R)\text{.}\) Then the map \(\phi: G\to G'\) defined by \(\phi(I_2)=I_2\) is clearly a homomorphism, but note that \(G\) is abelian while \(G'\) is not.
