Example7.2.8

Let \(G\) and \(G'\) be groups and let \(\phi\) be a homomorphism from \(G\) to \(G'\text{.}\) Then \(\Ker \phi\) is a normal subgroup of \(G\text{.}\)

Proof

Let \(K=\Ker \phi\text{.}\) We first show that \(K\) is a subgroup of \(G\text{.}\) Clearly, the identity element of \(G\) is in \(K\text{,}\) so \(K\neq \emptyset\text{.}\) Next, let \(k,m\in K\text{.}\) Then, letting \(e'\) denote the identity element of \(G'\text{,}\) we have \begin{equation*} \phi(km^{-1})=\phi(k)\phi(m)^{-1}=e'(e')^{-1}=e', \end{equation*} so \(km^{-1}\in K\text{.}\) Thus, by the 3.2.5, we have that \(K\) is a subgroup of \(G\text{.}\)

Next, let \(k\in K\) and let \(a\in G\text{.}\) Then \begin{equation*} \phi(aka^{-1})=\phi(a)\phi(k)\phi(a)^{-1}=\phi(a)e'\phi(a)^{-1}=\phi(a)\phi(a)^{-1}=e'. \end{equation*}

So \(aka^{-1}\in K\text{.}\) Thus, \(K \unlhd G\text{.}\)
