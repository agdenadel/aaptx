Proof

Suppose \(f\) has inverse \(g\text{.}\) Let \(t\in T\text{.}\) Then \(f(g(t))=t\text{,}\) so \(f\) is onto. Next, suppose \(s_1,s_2\in S\) with \(f(s_1)=f(s_2)\text{.}\) Then \begin{equation*}s_1=g(f(s_1))=g(f(s_2))=s_2,\end{equation*} so \(f\)is one-to-one.

Conversely, suppose \(f\) is a bijection. Then for every \(t\in T\text{,}\) there exists a unique element \(s_t\in S\) such that \(f(s_t)=t\text{.}\) It is then straighforward to show that the function \(g:T\to S\) defined by \begin{equation*}g(t)=t_s \text{ for every }t\in T\end{equation*} is an inverse of \(f\text{.}\)
