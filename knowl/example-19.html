Example2.6.20

Let \(F\) be the set of all functions from \(\R\) to \(\R\text{,}\) and define pointwise addition \(+\) on \(F\) by

\begin{equation*} (f+g)(x)=f(x)+g(x) \end{equation*}

for all \(f,g\in F\) and \(x\in \R\text{.}\) We claim that \(F\) is a group under pointwise addition. (For variety, in this proof we don't explicitly refer to \(\G_1\)â€“\(\G_3\text{,}\) though we certainly do verify they hold.) Indeed, if \(f,g\in F\) then clearly \(f+g\) is also a function from \(\R\) to \(\R\text{,}\) so \(F\) is closed under \(+\text{.}\)

Next, let \(f,g,h\in F\text{.}\) Then for all \(x\in \R\text{,}\)

\begin{align*} ((f+g)+h)(x)\amp =(f+g)(x)+h(x)\amp \amp\\ \amp =((f(x)+g(x))+h(x)\amp \amp\\ \amp =f(x)+(g(x)+h(x))\amp \amp \text{ (since addition is associative on \(\R\)) }\\ \amp =f(x)+(g+h)(x)\amp \amp\\ \amp =(f+(g+h))(x)\amp \amp . \end{align*}

Note that the key fact used in this argument is that \(f(x)\text{,}\) \(g(x)\) and \(h(x)\) all lie in \(\R\text{,}\) and addition is associative on \(\R\text{.}\) When you get used to such arguments, it is sufficient to say that associativity of \(+\) on \(F\) is inherited from the associativity of addition on \(\R\text{.}\)

Next, let \(z:\R\to\R\) be the function \(z(x)=0\) for all \(x\text{.}\) Then for all \(f\in F\) and \(x\in \R\text{,}\)

\begin{align*} (f+z)(x)\amp =f(x)+z(x)\amp \amp\\ \amp =f(x)+0\amp \amp\\ \amp =f(x)\amp \amp \\ \amp =0+f(x)\amp \amp\\ \amp =z(x)+f(x)\amp \amp\\ \amp = (z+f)(x). \end{align*}

So \(z\) is an identity element of \(\langle F,+\rangle\text{.}\)

Finally, let \(f\in F\text{,}\) and define \(g\in F\) by \(g(x)=-f(x)\) for all \(x\in \R\text{.}\) It is easy then to see that \(g\) is an inverse for \(f\) in \(F\text{.}\)

Hence, \(F\) is a group under pointwise addition. Note that it is uncountably infinite and abelian.
