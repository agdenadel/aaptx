We know from TheoremÂ 2.4.5 that \(\langle SL(n,\R),\cdot\rangle\) is a binary structure,and we know that matrix multiplication is always associative. Next, since \(\det I_n=1\text{,}\) \(I_n\) is in \(SL(n,\R)\text{,}\) and clearly acts as an identity element in \(SL(n,\R)\text{.}\) Finally, let \(A\in SL(n,\R)\text{.}\) Since \(\det A=1\neq 0\text{,}\) \(A\) has an inverse matrix \(A^{-1}\) in \(GL(n, \R)\text{.}\) \(A^{-1}\) is in \(SL(n,\R)\) since

\begin{equation*} \det(A^{-1})=\frac{1}{\det A}=1/1=1. \end{equation*}

So \(A\) has an inverse in \(SL(n,\R)\text{.}\)

Thus, our proof is complete.
