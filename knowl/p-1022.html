First, for every \(x \in \Z\text{,}\), \(n\) divides \(0=x-x\text{;}\); so \(x\equiv_n x\) for every \(x\in \Z\text{.}\). Thus, \(\equiv_n\) is reflexive. Next, let \(x,y \in \Z\) with \(x\equiv_n y\text{.}\). Then there exists some \(q\in \Z\) with \(x-y=nq\text{;}\); so \(y-x=n(-q)\text{.}\). Since \(-q\in \Z\text{,}\), this shows us that \(y\equiv_n x\text{.}\). Hence, \(\equiv_n\) is symmetric. Finally, let \(x,y\) and \(z\) be in \(\Z\) with \(x\equiv_n y\) and \(y\equiv_nz\text{.}\). Then there exist \(q_1,q_2 \in \Z\) with \(x-y=nq_1\) and \(y-z=nq_2\text{.}\). Thus, \begin{equation*} x-z=(x-y)+(y-z)=nq_1+nq_2=n(q_1+q_2); \end{equation*} since \(q_1+q_2 \in \Z\text{,}\), this shows us that \(x\equiv_n z\text{.}\). Thus, \(\equiv_n\) is transitive. Hence, \(\equiv_n\) is an equivalence relation, as desired.
