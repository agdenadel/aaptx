Example3.3.16

The groups \(\Z\) and \(\Q\) are not isomorphic. We use contradiction to prove this. Suppose that \(\Z\) and \(\Q\) are isomorphic via isomorphism \(\phi :\Q \to \Z\text{.}\) Let \(a\in \Q\text{.}\) Then \(a/2 \in \Q\) with \(a/2 + a/2 =a\text{.}\) Then \begin{equation*} \phi(a/2)+\phi(a/2)= \phi(a/2+a/2)=\phi(a); \end{equation*} since \(\phi(a/2)\) is in \(\Z\text{,}\) \(\phi(a)\) must be evenly divisible by 2. But \(a\) was arbitrary in \(\Q\) and \(\phi\) is onto, so this means every element of \(\Z\) must be evenly divisible by 2, which is clearly false. Thus, \(\Z\not\simeq \Q\text{.}\)
