Section7.4Exercises

1

How many distinct partitions of the set \(S=\{a,b,c,d\}\) are there? You do not need to list them. (Yes, you can find this answer online. But I recommend doing the work yourself for practice working with partitions!)

Solution

There are 15 distinct partitions of the set \(\{a,b,c,d\}\text{:}\)

\begin{equation*} \{\{a\},\{b\},\{c\},\{d\}\}, \{\{a,b\},\{c\},\{d\}\},\{\{a,c\},\{b\},\{d\}\},\{\{a,d\},\{b\},\{c\}\}, \end{equation*} \begin{equation*} \{\{b,c\},\{a\},\{d\}\},\{\{b,d\},\{a\},\{c\}\},\{\{c,d\},\{a\},\{b\}\}, \end{equation*} \begin{equation*} \{\{a,b\},\{c,d\}\}, \{\{a,c\},\{b,d\}\},\{\{a,d\},\{b,c\}\}, \end{equation*} \begin{equation*} \{\{a,b,c\},\{d\}\},\{\{a,b,d\},\{c\}\},\{\{a,c,d\},\{b\}\},\{\{b,c,d\},\{a\},\}, \end{equation*} \begin{equation*} \{\{a,b,c,d\}\}. \end{equation*}
2

  1. Let \(n\in \Z^+\text{.}\) Prove that \(\equiv_n\) is an equivalence relation on \(\Z\text{.}\)

  2. The cells of the induced partition of \(\Z\) are called the residue classes (or congruence classes) of \(\Z\) modulo \(n\). Using set notation of the form \(\{\ldots,\#, \#,\#,\ldots\}\) for each class, write down the residue classes of \(\Z\) modulo \(4\text{.}\)

Solution

  1. First, for every \(x \in \Z\text{,}\) \(n\) divides \(0=x-x\text{;}\) so \(x\equiv_n x\) for every \(x\in \Z\text{.}\) Thus, \(\equiv_n\) is reflexive. Next, let \(x,y \in \Z\) with \(x\equiv_n y\text{.}\) Then there exists some \(q\in \Z\) with \(x-y=nq\text{;}\) so \(y-x=n(-q)\text{.}\) Since \(-q\in \Z\text{,}\) this shows us that \(y\equiv_n x\text{.}\) Hence, \(\equiv_n\) is symmetric. Finally, let \(x,y\) and \(z\) be in \(\Z\) with \(x\equiv_n y\) and \(y\equiv_nz\text{.}\) Then there exist \(q_1,q_2 \in \Z\) with \(x-y=nq_1\) and \(y-z=nq_2\text{.}\) Thus,

    \begin{equation*} x-z=(x-y)+(y-z)=nq_1+nq_2=n(q_1+q_2); \end{equation*}

    since \(q_1+q_2 \in \Z\text{,}\) this shows us that \(x\equiv_n z\text{.}\) Thus, \(\equiv_n\) is transitive. Hence, \(\equiv_n\) is an equivalence relation, as desired.

  2. \begin{align*} \{\ldots,-8,-4,0,4,\ldots\},\amp \{\ldots,-7,-3,1,5,\ldots\},\\ \{\ldots,-6,-2,2,6,\ldots\},\amp \{\ldots,-5,-1,3,7,\ldots\} \end{align*}
3

Let \(G\) be a group with subgroup \(H\text{.}\) Prove that \(\simr\) is an equivalence relation on \(G\text{.}\)

Solution

First, let \(a\in G\text{.}\) Then \(aa^{-1}=e\in H\text{,}\) so \(a\simr a\text{.}\) Thus, \(\simr\) is reflexive.

Next, let \(a,b\in G\) with \(a\simr b\text{.}\) Then \(ab^{-1}\in H\text{,}\) so, since \(H\) is a subgroup of \(G\text{,}\) \((ab^{-1})^{-1}\in H\text{.}\) But \((ab^{-1})^{-1}=ba^{-1}\text{;}\) thus, \(b\simr a\text{,}\) and so \(\simr\) is symmetric.

Finally, let \(a,b,c\in G\) with \(a\simr b\) and \(b\siml c\text{.}\) Then \(ab^{-1}\) and \(bc^{-1}\) are in \(H\text{.}\) Since \(H\) is a subgroup of \(G\text{,}\) we must then have \((ab^{-1})(bc^{-1})\in H\text{;}\) but \((ab^{-1})(bc^{-1})\) equals \(ac^{-1}\text{.}\) Thus, \(a\simr c\text{,}\) and so \(\simr\) is transitive.

Hence, \(\simr\) is an equivalence relation on \(G\text{,}\) as desired.
4

For each subgroup \(H\) of group \(G\text{,}\) (i) find the left and the right cosets of \(H\) in \(G\text{,}\) (ii) decide whether or not \(H\) is normal in \(G\text{,}\) and (iii) find \((G:H)\text{.}\)

Write all permutations using disjoint cycle notation, and write all dihedral group elements using standard form.

  1. \(H=6\Z\) in \(G=2\Z\)

  2. \(H=\langle 4\rangle\) in \(\Z_{20}\)

  3. \(H=\langle (23)\rangle\) in \(G=S_3\)

  4. \(H=\langle r\rangle\) in \(G=D_4\)

  5. \(H=\langle f\rangle\) in \(G=D_4\)

Solution

    1. The left and the right cosets of \(6\Z\) in \(2\Z\) are \(6\Z\text{,}\) \(2+6\Z=6\Z+2\text{,}\) and \(4+6\Z=6\Z+4\text{.}\)

    2. \(6\Z \unlhd 2\Z\text{.}\)

    3. \((2\Z:6\Z)=3\text{.}\)

    1. The left and the right cosets of \(H=\langle 4\rangle =\{0,4,8,12,16\}\) in \(\Z_{20}\) are \(H\text{,}\) \(1+H=\{1,5,9,13,17\}=H+1\text{,}\) \(2+H=\{2,7,10,14,18\}=H+2\text{,}\) and \(3+H=\{3,8,11,15,19\}=H+3\text{.}\)

    2. \(H \unlhd \Z_{20}\text{.}\)

    3. \((\Z_{20}:H)=4\text{.}\)

    1. The left cosets of \(H\) in \(S_3\) are \(H=\{e,(23)\}\text{,}\) \((12)H=\{(12),(123)\}\text{,}\) and \((13)H=\{(13),(132)\}\text{.}\) The right cosets of \(H\) in \(S_3\) are \(H\text{,}\) \(H(12)=\{(12),(132)\}\text{,}\) and \(H(13)=\{(13),(123)\}\text{.}\)

    2. \(H\) is not normal in \(S_3\text{.}\)

    3. \((S_3:H)=3\text{.}\)

    1. The left cosets and the right cosets of \(H\) in \(D_4\) are \(H=\{e,r,r^2,r^3\}\) and \(fH=\{f,fr, fr^2, fr^3\}=Hf\text{.}\)

    2. \(H\unlhd D_4\text{.}\)

    3. \((D_4:H)=2\text{.}\)

    1. The left cosets of \(H\) in \(D_4\) are \(H=\{e,f\}\text{,}\) \(rH=\{r,fr^3\}\text{,}\) \(r^2H=\{r^2,fr^2\}\text{,}\) and \(r^3H=\{r^3, fr\}\text{.}\) The right cosets of \(H\) in \(D_4\) are \(H\text{,}\) \(Hr=\{r, fr\}\text{,}\) \(Hr^2=\{r^2,fr^2\}\text{,}\) and \(Hr^3=\{r^3, fr^3\}\text{.}\)

    2. \(H\) is not normal in \(D_4\text{.}\)

    3. \((D_4:H)=4\text{.}\)
5

For each of the following, give an example of a group \(G\) with a subgroup \(H\) that matches the given conditions. If no such example exists, prove that.

  1. A group \(G\) with subgroup \(H\) such that \(|G/H|=1\text{.}\)

  2. A finite group \(G\) with subgroup \(H\) such that \(|G/H|=|G|\text{.}\)

  3. An abelian group \(G\) of order \(8\) containing a non-normal subgroup \(H\) of order 2.

  4. A group \(G\) of order 8 containing a normal subgroup of order \(2\text{.}\)

  5. A nonabelian group \(G\) of order 8 containing a normal subgroup of index \(2\text{.}\)

  6. A group \(G\) of order 8 containing a subgroup of order \(3\text{.}\)

  7. An infinite group \(G\) containing a subgroup \(H\) of finite index.

  8. An infinite group \(G\) containing a finite nontrivial subgroup \(H\text{.}\)

Solution

(Other answers are possible.)

  1. \(G=H=S_3\text{.}\)

  2. \(G=S_3\text{,}\) \(H=\{e\}\text{.}\)

  3. No such example exists, since every subgroup of an abelian group is normal.

  4. \(G=\Z_8\text{,}\) \(H=\{0,4\}\text{.}\)

  5. \(G=D_4\text{,}\) \(H=\langle r\rangle\text{.}\)

  6. No such example exists, since by Lagrange's Theorem \(|H|\) must divide \(|G|\text{,}\) and 3 doesn't divide 8.

  7. \(G=\Z\text{,}\) \(H=2\Z\text{.}\)

  8. \(G=GL(2,\R)\text{,}\) \(H=\{\pm I_2\}\text{.}\)

7

Find the indices of:

  1. \(H=\langle (15)(24)\rangle\) in \(S_5\)

  2. \(K=\langle (1453)(25)\rangle\) in \(S_5\)

  3. \(L=\langle (2354)(34)\rangle\) in \(S_6\)

  4. \(A_n\) in \(S_n\)

Solution

  1. \((S_5:H)=|S_5|/|H|=120/2=60.\)

  2. In disjoint cycle notation, the permutation \((1453)(25)\) is written \((14523)\text{,}\) so \(K\) has order 5. Thus, \((S_5:K)=|S_5|/|K|=120/5=24.\)

  3. In disjoint cycle notation, the permutation \((2354)(34)\) is written \((23)(45)\text{,}\) so \(L\) has order 2. Thus, \((S_6:L)=|S_6|/|L|=720/2=360.\)

  4. \((S_n:A_n)=2\text{.}\)
8

Let \(G\) be a group of order \(pq\text{,}\) where \(p\) and \(q\) are prime, and let \(H\) be a proper subgroup of \(G\text{.}\) Prove that \(H\) is cyclic.

Solution

By Lagrange's Theorem, \(|H|\) divides \(pq\text{,}\) and since \(H\) is proper, \(|H|\neq pq\text{.}\) Thus, \(|H|=1,p\text{,}\) or \(q\text{.}\) Since the order of \(H\) is either 1 or a prime number, \(H\) must be cyclic.
9

Prove Corollary 7.3.9: that is, let \(G\) be a group of prime order, and prove that \(G\) is cyclic.

Solution

Since \(|G|\) is prime, \(|G|>1\text{,}\) so there exists \(a\in G\) with \(a\neq e\text{.}\) Then \(\langle a\rangle\) is a subgroup of \(G\text{,}\) so by Lagrange's Theorem, \(|\langle a\rangle |\) divides \(|G|\text{.}\) But \(|G|\) is prime and \(a\neq e\text{,}\) so we must have \(|\langle a\rangle |=|G|\text{;}\) thus, \(G=\langle a\rangle\text{.}\)
